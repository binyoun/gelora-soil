맥maek / pulse

The pulse altar and the seed: one work in three parts, grown on a held heartbeat.

grows on heart-rate settling breathes on each systole dies when you let go

You are read by your own hand: the palm as a full body map, a pulse sensor at the heart point. Your portrait grows as a doomed flower on your heartbeat. You hold its warm, beating life as a porcelain seed against your chest, feel it cool and stop when the flower dies, and leave the cold seed in a bowl that fills, across the exhibition, with everyone's finished bloom. The flower's life is bound to the pulse, the one signal whose stopping the whole genre of vanitas mourns.

the whole

Three parts, one system

b · the site

The altar

A low blackened plinth with a cast palm rest. A brass or 백자 porcelain dome at the heart point holds the pulse sensor. Above, on holographic gauze, your flower grows from your actual hand into the room, seen by everyone behind you.

f · the held body

The seed

A palm-sized kiln-fired porcelain seed you cup at the altar. It finds your pulse, warms, and beats the flower's rhythm against your chest. When the bloom dies it cools and stills in your hand.

the memento mori

The bowl

You set the cold seed among the others by the exit. The bowl fills through the show with every visitor's cooled bloom: the skull on the table, made collective and physical.

the encounter

What happens, in order

Your face is taken

At the altar a brief self-timer captures your portrait on a small front lens: the face the flower will wear. Then the piece turns to the fixed camera watching your hand.

You offer a hand

You rest one open hand palm-up on the dome, or take a cool seed from the bowl and cup it. The gesture is offering a pulse to be read, not pressing a button.

The pulse is found

The sensor catches your heartbeat. Stillness and a settled, slow heart let the flower take root faster: care, made measurable.

Your portrait blooms

The flower grows above your hand wearing your own face on its petals, breathing outward on every systole, glowing a little brighter on the beat.

You hold it to your chest

The seed is warm and beating the flower's rhythm. As the bloom matures the rhythm drifts from yours: it becomes a separate life that is still you.

It is let go

Lift the hand, or its life runs out, and the flower dies: not on a timer but because the tie to the living body broke. The seed's beat slows, stops, and it cools in your hand.

You leave it in the bowl

You set the cold seed down among the others. Nothing is stored. The only trace is a cooled stone in a growing pile.

the sound

The auscultation

Sound concept A: near-silence in three layers, all synthesised and driven by the flower's state. Vanitas as the return to silence. Prototyped now, running in the browser.

Three layers

The felt thumpA low kick under each heartbeat, felt through a transducer in the plinth more than heard.
on beat → low sine, calm sets weight
The celadon bellA single struck partial on the beat once the flower has taken, brighter and denser as it matures.
maturity → note window + overtones
The droneA sustained bed that fades in with maturity, detunes sour as the flower wilts, and falls to silence when you let go.
wilt → detune + darken · released → silence

Yours, or a collaborator's

Layer A is parameter-mapped generative sound, your own medium, kept intimate and restrained. A collaborator earns their place later, and specifically:

  • 맥 as 장단. A percussionist or composer who knows Korean rhythmic cycles makes the pulse musically true, with 장구 as the single voice, threading toward the four-hands flagship where four instruments become four visitors.
  • Spatial sound. Multichannel or Ambisonic so the bloom is heard growing in the room, for ISEA or Ars Electronica scale.
  • The bowl drone. A collective near-silent swell as each cold seed joins the pile.
the seed

The object, and the honest hard parts

Inside the porcelain

kiln-fired 백자 shell (bone / relic)
ESP32-C3 · BLE
LRA + DRV2605 · haptic beat
PTC heater + thermistor · 34-38C
MAX30102 or contact pulse
Qi inductive charging (no port)

A two-part seed: the shell is fired first, a potted electronics core is sealed in after. Sealed and wipeable, it charges in the bowl between visitors.

What is genuinely hard

  • Power and heat. A heater on a palm-sized cell sets the bloom length; the answer is gentle warmth, short blooms, fast inductive top-up.
  • Hygiene. Sealed porcelain, wiped between hands, enough seeds in rotation.
  • Fabrication. You cannot fire ceramic around electronics, so a ceramicist is the natural first collaborator.
  • Loss. Track seeds by device presence only, and accept some as the cost of an edition people hold.
